AIM

Reduce unnecessary trips to the GP and hospital by helping clients diagnose their health issue on the basis of the symptoms and family background history through the use of an online tool.

Goal 1: Utilise, process and breakdown raw data on previous anonymous data on heath diagnosis.

  • Raw data on previous health diagnoses and other data will have to be analysed and linked up with its common symptoms, and then to the medical condition they are related with.

    • Goal 2: Developing an interface that is user friendly to people of various technical and health knowledge.

  • To achieve this goal will require firsthand experience in both using and developing UI designs for both web and mobile.

    • Goal 3: Developing a system which can handle various issues, symptoms and family background history which is then analysed and returns a possible health diagnosis.

  • The teams knowledge of databases and programming will be helpful in assisting the ability to link up data, and turn the raw data into information.

    • PLANS AND PROGRESS

    Our project is a health issue prediction tool which will be developed as a web-based application. Major parts of this application would be based off artificial intelligence learning and data mining.

    We had the idea to create a health issue prediction tool after seeing how much misinformation there is online. Many people go online to search up symptoms they may have a hard time trying to find out what the cause may be, but there is lots of inaccurate information which is provided by people who are not medical professionals. By creating this health issue prediction tool based off of science, and with the help of medical professionals, we would be able to ensure that people have the best information available to them.

    We identified the stakeholders during the analysis stage of the project. We did this in order to identify our target users so we could then create personas (such as a young user, mid aged user, and elderly user, user without IT knowledge, etc.). By knowing our target users, we can identify what elements need to be incorporated and excluded from the project. For example, if we identify a 20 year old female who uses technology regularly and is able to learn now systems easily (assuming they are user friendly) we can then test the application during various stages to see how that user would ideally interact with the system. From there, we can determine if something is too complicated or confusing, as well as identifying what works well.

    In addition to identifying the stakeholders we planned to create context diagrams which relate to the stakeholders and how they would interact with the application, data flow diagrams to identify what data the application would be processing, such as recent blood test information, body temperature, etc. The data flow diagrams determine where data will go and how it will be used by both the user and the system. To further identify inputs and outputs we wanted to create an IPO chart to show how inputs from users will be processed and how they will then be shown back to the user. An example is a user entering their temperature e.g. 38°C, an IPO chart will show the developers how that data goes from '38°C' into a suggestion that they may have a high fever, the process demonstrates how the system determines that answer. /p>

    SCOPE AND LIMITS

    Scope

    A major feature of the development of this application includes its ability to receive an input from the user about their symptoms and family history. This is the most important part, as it will help to determine the possible medical condition, thus, it is crucial that this part is fully developed and also tested. This ensures that the service is fully functional and accurate. As the development of this feature will take the most time, it is not feasible to add other features such as extracting data from other services such as myHealthRecord.


    Limits and Constraints

    Economic Constraints

  • Cost: As this might be a start-up company, the cost of employees and IT equipment would be a limiting factor.

  • Time: Even though more employees means that the project might be able to finish earlier, it also increases the cost. But at the same time, the process of building a database, processing and manipulating data, as well as testing, takes a lot of time and resources.

    • Technical Constraints

  • Speed of processing: The solution should be hosted on a server that has at least 6 cores of processing power and 16gb of ram. The network should also be able to maintain a high upload and download speed.

  • Availability of equipment and services: The location of the database and server could result in it not getting access to the highest network speed, due to availability and demand. Also, some network and computers could not be available due to supply/demand issues, or due to inflated prices.

  • Security: The solution should be able to verify users through a system like myGov, such as autofilling data from a service like myHealthRecord.

  • Compatibility: The solution should be designed as being able to run on different platforms, screen sizes, and resolutions. The may be hindered by either technical expertise, time, or cost constraints.

    • Social Constraints

  • Level of expertise amongst users: Some users of the service may be adults with no technical expertise or knowledge of health-related forms. So its important to cater the solution to users of all technical abilities.

    • Legal Constraints

  • Privacy: Any data collected or processed should not breach local or forge on privacy laws. In regards to local laws, the service provided must not breach the Privacy Act 1988, Health Record Act 2001, and the Privacy and Data Protection Act 2014.

  • Ownership: The product will be owned by the compant developing the service but this also means that there will be legal hurdles, such as filing patents and enforcing copyright. This would also bring responsibilities for any mistakes or errors in the future.

    • Usability Constraints

  • Accessiblity: People who may have a disability hindering them from utilising the service to the full extent. For example, if a user has a visual disability, the website may need to adapt by increasing font sizes or having a voice over function.

  • Ease of use: The solution should be easy to navigate and learn. Hence, the application should be able to provide hints and tips along the way. But, it should also be designed in a way where the user can reasonably move throughout different parts of the service without any help.

    TESTING

    To ensure that the application performs as expected, its important to develop a variety of tests. These tests can be specific to a particular function, such as returning a predicted health issue based on symptoms that are the most probable for that illness. As the application heavily relies on data, particularly the relationships between data, to find correlations and patterns to determine which symptoms are the most common for which particular disease, whilst also factoring in other variables such as age and gender. It's important to note that data cannot always be 100% accurate; correlation does not mean causation, but the application can still attempt to provide the most probable prediction by analysing the data available.

    An effective method to test the program could be to provide a prototype to individuals who do suffer from some kind of disease or illness, ask them to take note of their symptoms and personal information (age, gender), and compare their actual disease with the predicted one. If their actual disease was part of the predicted list (perhaps there could be a percentage probability for several diseases) and its high up on the list, then that could be considered a success. As is with any project, the most important person is the end user, so its important to allow them to test the application instead of just the developers.

    RISKS

    No matter how well you think you've planned a project, anything can go wrong and the entire project can run into problems. So, when a project is being planned, its important to identity possible risks and implement possible plans for when you have to deal with these risks. Risks can fall into several categories such as cost / financial, technical, people, and even in some cases the client.

    In relation to our project idea, our web-based application requires a range of software tools as outlined in Tools and Technologies, and these each come with their associated risks.

    The fact that our project's main function is to give people an accurate and reliable assessment of possible health conditions that they may have. This in itself is a major risk, as giving a completely incorrect result could have severe effects on the client (the user), including shock and anxiety from an incorrect prediction, which could then result in backlash on the business / developers.

    In order for our application to give accurate and reliable predictions, it needs to have access to data and statistics for the majority of known health conditions, illnesses, and diseases. This can be risky, as not only will this most likely be very expensive to access, but it will also need to be updated on a regular basis to account for new diseases.

    Databases will play a major role in the success of our application, but unfortunately, maintaining the integrity of these databases can be difficult in some cases. This can be several reasons, ranging from the difficulty to learn the software / programming languages, and ensuring that people (employees) have the skills to operate / manage such a database, which can result in a non-functional application.

    As the user will be required to enter some personal information, such as age, sex, and any other past health conditions, as well as other optional information, this can result in legal risks, as the developers need to ensure that the privacy and security of the confidential information is of a high standard and obeys regulations.

    Schedule risks are another common risk in many projects, and ours is no exception. Some activities may very well take longer than we predicted on our Timeframe, and this can result in many other risk factors becoming a possibility, including increased cost. If the project has a deadline and scheduling becomes an issue, then this could result in a performance risk as the application may fail to produce the results that were outlined in the specifications / requirements.
